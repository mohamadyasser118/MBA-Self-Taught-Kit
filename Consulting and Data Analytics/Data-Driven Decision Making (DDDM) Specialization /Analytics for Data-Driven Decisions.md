# Module 1 — Applying Analytics to Implement Solutions

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Before implementing solutions, organizations must identify root causes and select the best-fit solutions. Brainstorming generates ideas (round robin, popcorn). The Five Whys technique drills down to root causes. Fishbone (cause-and-effect) diagrams visually map potential causes. Multi-voting and impact matrices prioritize causes. The improvement selection matrix rates solutions against root causes (impact, cost, risk). Pilots and design of experiments (DOE) test solutions before full deployment. DOE systematically examines multiple factors and their interactions (factorial experiments) — more efficient than "one factor at a time."

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Brainstorming:** Generating wide range of ideas in non-critical, creative environment.
  - Round robin: each person shares one idea in turn
  - Popcorn: anyone shares anytime

- **Five Whys:** Ask "why" up to five times to drill down to root cause. Supported by team input and data.

- **Fishbone Diagram (Cause-and-Effect, Ishikawa):** Visually maps potential causes to a problem. Categories often include: Methods, Machines, Materials, Manpower, Measurement, Environment.

- **Multi-voting:** Teams prioritize causes after brainstorming. Each member votes; top vote-getters proceed.

- **Impact Matrix:** Ranks causes by significance (e.g., impact on customer, cost, safety). Guides focused data collection.

- **Improvement Selection Matrix:**
  - List root causes on horizontal axis
  - List potential solutions on vertical axis
  - Rate importance of root causes (1-10)
  - Rate solutions on impact, cost, risk (1-5 or 1-10)
  - Highest scoring solutions are candidates

- **Quick Wins:** Easy, cheap, reversible, within control. Implement immediately.

- **Pilot:** Small-scale test before full deployment.

- **Design of Experiments (DOE):** Systematic method to identify which factors most affect success metrics. Examines multiple factors and interactions simultaneously.

- **Factorial Experiment:** Varies multiple factors together. More efficient than "one factor at a time" (which misses interactions).

- **DOE Process:**
  1. State problem and define objectives
  2. Select responses and factors
  3. Design experiment
  4. Run experiment and collect data
  5. Analyze results

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The Five Whys is deceptively simple but effective.** The first answer is rarely the root cause. Keep asking.

2. **Fishbone diagrams organize thinking.** Categories (Methods, Machines, Materials, etc.) ensure broad exploration.

3. **The improvement selection matrix forces trade-offs.** Not all solutions are equal. Impact, cost, and risk must be balanced.

4. **DOE is superior to one-factor-at-a-time.** Varying multiple factors together reveals interactions that one-factor-at-a-time misses.

5. **Resistance to DOE exists, but wider adoption shows its value.** Data-driven decision-making requires experimentation.

6. **Quick wins build momentum.** Implement easy, cheap, reversible solutions immediately while working on long-term solutions.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Brainstorming (Round robin, Popcorn) | Idea generation | Early in problem-solving |
| Five Whys | Drill down to root cause | After problem identified |
| Fishbone Diagram | Visual cause mapping | Exploring potential causes |
| Multi-voting | Prioritization | After brainstorming |

| Improvement Selection Matrix | Rate solutions (impact, cost, risk) | Solution selection |
| Quick Wins | Easy, cheap, reversible | Immediate implementation |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

- **Improvement Selection Matrix Score:** Weighted sum of impact, cost (inverse), risk (inverse)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Do not jump to solutions. Identify root causes first.

- Use Five Whys with a team. Different perspectives reveal different root causes.

- After brainstorming, use multi-voting to focus. Do not try to solve everything.

- Use improvement selection matrix to compare solutions objectively. Do not rely on intuition alone.

- Test solutions with pilots or DOE before full deployment.

- Quick wins build momentum. Implement them immediately.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Five Whys is powerful but often misused. People stop at the first "why." The discipline of asking "why" five times forces deeper thinking.

The improvement selection matrix is a structured alternative to gut-feeling decisions. By rating impact, cost, and risk, organizations can compare solutions objectively.

DOE is underutilized in many organizations. The "one factor at a time" approach is inefficient and misses interactions. Factorial experiments are superior.

# Module 2 — Data-Driven Operational Excellence

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Operational excellence frameworks provide structured approaches to data-driven process improvement. ISO 9001:2015 provides a quality management system framework (context analysis, process mapping, KPIs, risk management, continuous improvement). Lean focuses on eliminating waste (eight types: correction, overproduction, processing, inventory, material movement, motion, waiting, intellect). Six Sigma focuses on reducing variation (DMAIC: Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control). The choice of methodology depends on organizational maturity: ISO for foundational processes, Lean for waste reduction, Six Sigma for variation reduction.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **ISO 9001:2015:** International standard for quality management systems (QMS). Focuses on meeting customer requirements through data-driven processes.

- **ISO 9001:2015 Key Elements:**
  - Understand business context (internal/external factors, interested parties)
  - Process mapping and KPIs
  - Leadership commitment (align KPIs with strategic goals)
  - Risk assessment (FMEA, SWOT, PEST)
  - Resource management (hardware/software inventories, calibration)
  - Continuous improvement

- **Lean Philosophy:** Value = good quality, zero defects, fair pricing, on-time delivery. Eliminate waste.

- **Eight Wastes (DOWNTIME):**
  - **Defects (Correction):** Fixing mistakes. Solution: mistake proofing.
  - **Overproduction:** Producing more than needed. Solution: single piece flow.
  - **Processing:** Unnecessary extra work. Solution: standardized work.
  - **Inventory:** Excess or idle materials. Solution: pull system.
  - **Motion:** Unnecessary worker movement. Solution: cellular flow layout.
  - **Waiting:** Delays between process steps. Solution: standardized work.
  - **Transportation:** Unnecessary material movement. Solution depends on root cause.
  - **Intellect:** Wrong person doing task. Solution: assign right person.

- **Six Sigma:** Statistical methodology to stabilize processes and reduce defects. Measures process capability using sigma (Z) levels.

- **Sigma Levels and Defects:**
  - 3 sigma: 66,807 errors per million (93.3% yield)
  - 4 sigma: 6,210 errors per million (99.38% yield)
  - 5 sigma: 233 errors per million (99.977% yield)
  - 6 sigma: 3.4 errors per million (99.9997% yield)

- **DMAIC (Six Sigma):**
  - **Define:** Problem statement, goals, team charter, process map
  - **Measure:** Collect data, refine problem statement
  - **Analyze:** Identify root causes and sources of variation
  - **Improve:** Develop, pilot, select solutions (financial viability)
  - **Control:** Validate improvements, sustain performance, document new practices

- **Choosing the Right Methodology:**
  - ISO: Lack structured processes, documentation, quality understanding
  - Lean: Inefficiencies (searching, waiting, delays)
  - Six Sigma: Persistent defects, overwhelming data, process variation

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **ISO provides a foundation for organizations without structured processes.** It forces documentation, process mapping, and quality thinking.

2. **Lean is about waste elimination.** Value stream mapping quantifies waste. Reducing waste improves flow and customer value.

3. **Six Sigma is about variation reduction.** Variation is the enemy of quality. Control charts and DMAIC drive improvement.

4. **The choice of methodology depends on organizational maturity.** Start with ISO for foundation, then Lean for waste, then Six Sigma for variation.

5. **Six Sigma (Z=6) achieves 99.9997% quality (3.4 defects per million).** Improving from 3.8 sigma to 6 sigma dramatically reduces errors in healthcare, aviation, and manufacturing.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| ISO 9001:2015 | Quality management system | Building foundational processes |
| Lean | Waste elimination (8 wastes) | Inefficiency, delays, excess inventory |
| Six Sigma | Variation reduction (DMAIC) | Persistent defects, high variation |
| DMAIC | Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control | Six Sigma projects |
| Value Stream Mapping | Visualize material and information flow | Identifying waste |
| Eight Wastes (DOWNTIME) | Defects, Overproduction, Processing, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Transportation, Intellect | Waste identification |
| Sigma Level | Measure of process capability | Quality measurement |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Sigma Level:** Number of standard deviations between process mean and nearest specification limit

- **Defects Per Million Opportunities (DPMO):** (Number of defects / Opportunities) × 1,000,000

- **Yield:** 1 - (Defects / Opportunities)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- If your organization lacks structured processes, start with ISO 9001.

- If you have inefficiencies (waiting, searching, delays), use Lean and value stream mapping.

- If you have persistent defects or high variation, use Six Sigma and DMAIC.

- Use the eight wastes framework (DOWNTIME) to identify improvement opportunities.

- Set sigma targets based on industry requirements. Medical devices and aviation require higher sigma than retail.

Six Sigma's 3.4 defects per million is the gold standard. But many processes do not need 6 sigma. Medical devices and aviation do. Retail may not. The appropriate sigma level depends on risk.

# Module 3 — Applying Data-Driven Decisions

## A. Core Idea of the Module

A data literacy culture requires buy-in from all members, leadership communication of data vision, experimentation, and learning from failure. Data ethics and privacy are shared responsibilities: data privacy is legal compliance (GDPR, CCPA); data ethics is how organizations choose to handle data responsibly. Industry case studies demonstrate applications: hospitality (casino customer segmentation, revenue decline analysis), manufacturing (centralized data warehouse, 30% forecast accuracy improvement), e-commerce (market basket analysis, cross-selling recommendations).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Data Literacy Culture:** All members can ask questions, explore data, develop creative solutions. Requires leadership vision, experimentation, learning from failure.

- **Building Data Literacy Culture:**
  - Leadership communicates data vision
  - Encourage experimentation and learning from failure
  - In-house roundtables and cross-functional feedback sessions
  - External conferences and user group meetups
  - Google's 20 percent project (creative side projects)

- **Data Privacy:** Legal requirements (GDPR, CCPA) protecting personal information.

- **Data Ethics:** How organizations choose to handle data responsibly (beyond legal compliance).

- **Data Security Best Practices:**
  - Anonymize data
  - Keep software updated
  - Strong, unique passwords (do not share)
  - Encryption technologies
  - Ethics committees
  - Information security analysts

- **Hospitality Case Study (Casino):**
  - Problem: revenue decline despite increased guest visits
  - Segmentation: new, returning, maintained, vanished guests
  - Finding: maintained guests spending less and visiting less frequently
  - Deeper segmentation: medium spenders reduced trip frequency
  - Geographic spend data revealed competitors gaining high-value customers
  - Action: targeted marketing to medium spenders

- **Manufacturing Case Study:**
  - Problem: inconsistent data storage, separate Excel files, non-standardized orders
  - Solution: new POS system, centralized data warehouse (sales, machine sensors, inventory)
  - Benefit: 30% increase in forecast accuracy, reduced order lead time (1 week), fewer stockouts

- **E-commerce Case Study (Market Basket Analysis - MBA):**
  - Identifies items frequently purchased together
  - Metrics: confidence, lift
  - Applications: cross-selling recommendations (Amazon), merchandising, targeted marketing, coupon strategies

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Data literacy is not just for analysts.** All employees must be able to ask questions and explore data.

2. **Experimentation and failure are part of data culture.** Google's 20 percent project encourages innovation.

3. **Data privacy is legal; data ethics is moral.** Compliance is minimum; ethics is higher standard.

4. **Data breaches often result from user error (weak passwords, password sharing).** Individual behavior matters.

5. **Market basket analysis (MBA) is a simple but powerful technique.** Amazon uses it for cross-selling recommendations.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Data Literacy Culture | Organization-wide data capability | Building data-driven organization |
| Data Privacy (GDPR, CCPA) | Legal compliance | Handling personal information |
| Data Ethics | Responsible data handling (beyond compliance) | All data activities |
| Customer Segmentation | New, returning, maintained, vanished | Hospitality case study |
| Data Warehouse | Centralized data from multiple sources | Manufacturing case study |
| Market Basket Analysis (MBA) | Identifies items purchased together | E-commerce cross-selling |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Market Basket Analysis Metrics:**
  - **Confidence:** P(B|A) = P(A and B) / P(A)
  - **Lift:** Confidence / Expected confidence (P(B))

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Build data literacy through training, roundtables, and cross-functional projects.

- Encourage experimentation. Failure is learning.

- Data ethics is not optional. Organizations must go beyond legal compliance.

- Use market basket analysis for cross-selling recommendations, store layout, and coupon targeting.

- Centralize data from multiple sources into a data warehouse.

- [x] Analyze hospitality case study (casino segmentation)
- [x] Analyze manufacturing case study (centralized data warehouse)
- [x] Analyze e-commerce case study (market basket analysis)

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The casino case study is a great example of segmentation. The initial view (revenue down despite more guests) was puzzling. Segmentation revealed the problem: medium spenders in the "maintained guests" category were visiting less frequently. Further geographic analysis showed competitors were targeting them. Analytics without segmentation is blind.

Market basket analysis is simple but powerful. Amazon's "customers who bought this also bought that" is MBA. Lift and confidence metrics identify associations. But correlation is not causation — associations may be spurious.

Data literacy is the foundation. Organizations can have all the tools and data in the world, but if employees cannot ask questions or interpret results, nothing changes.
