This course is the second in the Human Resource Management: HR for People Managers specialization. It covers the essential processes of workforce planning, recruitment, selection, and onboarding. # Module 1 — Workforce Planning

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Workforce planning aligns recruitment with organizational strategy. This module covers the internal environment (turnover, operations, finances) and external environment (political, legal, economic, technological, demographic) that shape hiring needs. It also covers job analysis (gathering information about job duties) and job design (structuring jobs for efficiency, motivation, safety, and engagement). Key frameworks include the Job Characteristics Model (skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback) and the distinction between internal vs. external hiring.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Workforce Planning:** Linking workforce requirements directly to the business's strategic plan. Identifies current and future competency gaps.

- **Three Competitive Strategies (and required workforce behaviors):**
  - Operational Excellence (cost efficiency) → efficiency behaviors
  - Product Leadership (innovation) → creativity, innovation
  - Customer Intimacy (customized service) → problem-solving

- **Internal Environment Factors:** Turnover rates, sales projections, seasonal fluctuations, production capacity, financial health, manager availability.

- **External Environment Factors:** Political, legal, societal, economic, technological, demographic factors (PEST analysis).

- **Turnover:** The rate at which employees leave and are replaced. Causes include low engagement, lack of development, aging workforce.

- **Labor Shortage Options:** Temporary labor, independent contractors, reassign work to current employees, technology, reduce turnover, outsource.

- **Internal vs. External Hiring:**
  - Internal advantages: less expensive, motivates employees, develops specific skills
  - External advantages: new ideas, fresh perspectives, access to unique skills not available internally
  - Research shows internal hires perform better and are less likely to be fired

- **Job Analysis:** Gathering critical information about a job to create accurate job descriptions. Methods: job performance, observation, interviews, critical incident analysis, structured questionnaires, Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ).

- **Job Description Components:** Job title, reporting relationships, essential/non-essential functions, job specifications (knowledge, skills, abilities, education, physical conditions).

- **Job Design:** Planning the where, what, how, and when of job tasks. Four key aspects:
  - Efficiency (industrial engineering, lean)
  - Motivation (Job Characteristics Model)
  - Safety and health (ergonomics)
  - Job scope (enlargement, enrichment, rotation)

- **Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham):**
  - Skill variety
  - Task identity (completing a whole piece of work)
  - Task significance (impact on others)
  - Autonomy (independence)
  - Feedback (knowledge of results)

- **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA):** Requires defining essential job functions for accommodations. Bona fide occupational qualifications (BFOQ) allow discrimination only when necessary for job performance.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Recruitment must align with organizational strategy.** The workforce behaviors you need (efficiency, creativity, problem-solving) depend on your competitive strategy. Define competencies before recruiting.

2. **Internal hires perform better and are less likely to be fired than external hires,** despite external hires often receiving higher pay. Promote from within whenever possible.

3. **Job analysis is the foundation of all HR functions.** Without accurate job analysis, job descriptions, selection criteria, performance appraisals, and compensation are all flawed.

4. **Job design affects motivation.** The Job Characteristics Model (skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback) predicts intrinsic motivation. Jobs lacking these lead to disengagement.

5. **Amy's six tips for effective HR:**
   - Hire slowly, fire quickly
   - Align recruitment with business strategy
   - Build cross-departmental relationships (especially with finance)
   - Hire for gaps (skills or competencies you lack)
   - Use professional networks and existing resources
   - Use data and analytics to back decisions

## F. Practical Implications

- Before recruiting, conduct a job analysis. Do not rely on outdated job descriptions.
- Promote from within when possible — internal hires perform better and stay longer.
- For labor shortages, consider temporary labor or independent contractors before permanent hires. But correctly classify workers (IRS guidelines).
- Use the Job Characteristics Model to design jobs that motivate. Autonomy and feedback are especially powerful.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The finding that internal hires perform better and are less likely to be fired is striking given that external hires often receive higher pay. Organizations pay a premium for external talent that underperforms internal candidates. The solution is not to stop external hiring (new ideas matter) but to invest more in internal development and promotion.

The Job Characteristics Model is foundational. Jobs that lack autonomy, feedback, and task significance lead to disengagement. Many organizations design jobs poorly — not because they must, but because they have not thought about job design.

# Module 2 — Recruitment

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Recruitment is the process of attracting candidates. This module covers organizational decisions in recruitment (how many, who, when, where), the employer brand (how the organization is perceived by potential candidates), recruiter effectiveness (trustworthiness, professionalism), sourcing methods (internal, external, passive candidates, social media, mobile recruiting), and legal/ethical issues (honesty, cultural fit vs. diversity, avoiding discrimination).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Recruitment Decisions:** How many employees needed, who to recruit (internal/external/active/passive), when and where recruiting will take place, choice of recruiters (internal vs. outsourced), messaging and timing.

- **Targeted Recruiting:** Actively seeking candidates with specific characteristics, often to comply with affirmative action or address diversity issues.

- **Person-Environment (PE) Fit:** Matching candidates' values, goals, and personalities with the organization and job.

- **Employer Brand:** The organization's reputation as an employer. Influenced by size, age, profitability, product awareness, location, social media presence, culture.

- **Recruiter Effectiveness:** Effective recruiters are perceived as trustworthy, personable, and friendly. Training should include interview techniques, listening skills, diversity/sensitivity, legal knowledge, professionalism.

- **Passive Candidates:** Individuals not actively seeking jobs but possessing unique skills. Engaged through LinkedIn, email, employee referrals, and relationship-building.

- **Mobile Recruiting:** Over 90% of job candidates use mobile devices to search for jobs. Requires mobile-optimized career sites, streamlined applications, automated text messages.

- **Cultural Fit:** Alignment between candidate values and organizational culture. Balanced with diversity to avoid homogeneity.

- **Inclusion:** Creating a workplace where diverse employees feel welcomed, engaged, and productive — beyond just hiring diverse candidates.

- **Legal Considerations in Recruitment:** Honesty, avoiding discrimination, EEOC compliance, ADA accommodations.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Recruitment is marketing.** Organizations must manage their employer brand just as they manage product brands. A positive culture is a competitive advantage.

2. **Recruiter effectiveness matters.** Early impressions of recruiters influence candidates' decisions. Trustworthy, personable, professional recruiters attract better candidates.

3. **Passive candidates are often the best candidates.** They are not actively job-seeking but may be open to the right opportunity. Reaching them requires relationship-building, not just job postings.

4. **Mobile recruiting is no longer optional.** Over 90% of candidates use mobile devices. If your career site is not mobile-optimized, you will lose candidates.

5. **Cultural fit is important but must be balanced with diversity.** Too much focus on fit leads to homogeneity and groupthink. Zappos uses strong cultural fit screening; Google balances fit with multiple competencies.

6. **Ethical recruitment requires honesty.** Candidates should be told the truth about the job, the organization, and the hiring process. Imperfect information is common, but deliberate deception is unethical.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Targeted Recruiting | Seeking candidates with specific characteristics | Affirmative action, diversity hiring |
| Employer Brand | Organization's reputation as employer | Recruitment marketing |
| Recruiter Training | Interview techniques, listening, legal knowledge | Improving recruiter effectiveness |
| Passive Recruiting | Engaging candidates not actively seeking jobs | Hard-to-fill roles |
| Mobile Recruiting | Mobile-optimized career sites, text messages | Any recruitment (90%+ use mobile) |
| Cultural Fit Assessment | Personality inventories, targeted interview questions | Balancing fit with diversity |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Treat recruitment as marketing. Your employer brand matters. A strong culture is a recruiting advantage.

- Train recruiters. Unprofessional recruiters drive away good candidates.

- For hard-to-fill roles, recruit passive candidates. Use LinkedIn, employee referrals, and relationship-building.

- Optimize your career site for mobile. Candidates will drop off if the application is not mobile-friendly.

- Balance cultural fit with diversity. Do not hire clones. Seek candidates who add new perspectives.

- Be honest with candidates. Ethical recruitment builds trust and reduces turnover from mismatched expectations.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The passive candidate insight is important. The best candidates are often not looking. If you only post jobs and wait for applicants, you will miss them. Active sourcing (LinkedIn, networking, referrals) is essential.

The cultural fit vs. diversity tension is real. Zappos famously fires employees who do not fit their culture, even if they are good at their jobs. This creates a strong culture but risks homogeneity. Google's approach — assessing multiple competencies, including fit but not over-indexing — is more balanced.

# Module 3 — Selection

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Selection is the process of choosing the best candidate. This module covers the selection process (screening, testing, interviewing, reference checks, background checks, offer), selection criteria (balancing job fit and cultural fit), legal considerations (biases, background checks, negligent hiring, UGESP), and selection tools (structured interviews, work samples, cognitive ability tests, personality inventories). The module emphasizes using valid, reliable, and job-relevant selection methods.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Selection Process Steps:** Screening applications, testing, interviewing, reference checks, background checks, offer.

- **Selection Criteria:** Must balance job fit (skills, knowledge, abilities) and cultural fit (values, personality).

- **Google's Four Criteria:** General cognitive ability, leadership potential, cultural fit ("Googleyness"), role-related knowledge.

- **Biases in Interviewing:**
  - Confirmation bias: seek information confirming initial impression
  - In-group bias: favor candidates similar to interviewer
  - Contrast bias: evaluation distorted by order of interviews
  - Halo effect: one positive trait colors entire evaluation
  - Horn effect: one negative trait colors entire evaluation
  - Prejudice and discrimination: based on protected characteristics

- **Reliability:** Consistency of a test or experiment in producing the same results upon repeated trials.

- **Validity:** How well a test score relates to actual job performance.
  - Criterion-related validity: correlation between test scores and job performance
  - Content validity: testing actual job tasks
  - Construct validity: measuring traits (intelligence, leadership)

- **Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures (UGESP):** Require validity studies when there is adverse impact on protected groups.

- **Negligent Hiring:** Employer fails to check backgrounds and is liable for resulting damages.

- **Background Checks:** Should verify substance abuse, employment history, references, credit (where legal), education, driving records, criminal history. Must comply with FCRA, ADA, Ban the Box laws.

- **Top Predictors of Job Performance (validity ranking):**
  1. Work-sample tests
  2. General mental-ability (cognitive) tests
  3. Structured interviews
  4. Integrity tests, personality inventories (weaker)

- **Structured Interview:** All candidates asked same predetermined questions in same order. Uses situational, behavioral, or knowledge questions.

- **Multiple Hurdle Model:** Screens candidates through successive stages; eliminates those who do not meet criteria at each hurdle.

- **Compensatory Model:** Assigns values to candidate attributes; strengths in some areas offset weaknesses in others.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Do not ask "Tell me about yourself."** This open-ended question leads candidates to share irrelevant personal information (age, religion, family status) that can bias decisions and create legal risk.

2. **Work samples and cognitive ability tests are the best predictors of job performance.** Unstructured interviews and years of education are poor predictors.

3. **Structured interviews are far more valid than unstructured interviews.** Use the same questions for all candidates, in the same order, with consistent rating scales.

4. **Background checks are essential to avoid negligent hiring.** But criminal records should not automatically disqualify candidates — consider nature, recency, and relevance.

5. **Biases are pervasive but can be reduced.** Training, structured interviews, multiple interviewers, and external auditors help.

6. **Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity.** A test can be reliable (consistent) but not valid (measuring the wrong thing). Both are required.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Selection Process (7 steps) | Screening, testing, interviewing, references, background, offer | Every hire |
| Structured Interview | Same questions, same order, consistent rating | Best practice for interviewing |
| Work Sample Test | Candidate performs actual job tasks | Most valid predictor |
| Cognitive Ability Test | General mental ability | Second most valid predictor |
| Multiple Hurdle Model | Sequential elimination | High-volume hiring |
| Compensatory Model | Strengths offset weaknesses | Complex roles |
| Background Check | Employment, education, criminal, credit | After conditional offer |

---

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Replace unstructured interviews with structured interviews. Use situational or behavioral questions based on job analysis.

- Use work samples or cognitive ability tests where feasible. They are the most valid predictors.

- Do not ask "Tell me about yourself." It adds no job-relevant information and invites bias.

- Conduct background checks after conditional offer. Comply with FCRA, Ban the Box, and ADA.

- Train interviewers to recognize biases. Use multiple interviewers and external auditors where possible.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The finding that work samples and cognitive ability tests are the best predictors is robust across decades of research. Yet many organizations rely on unstructured interviews, which have very low validity. The gap between research and practice is large.

The "Tell me about yourself" question is ubiquitous but harmful. It invites irrelevant personal information (age, marital status, religion) that can bias decisions and create legal risk. There is no job-relevant reason to ask it. Stop using it.

Structured interviews feel mechanical, but they work. The same questions, the same order, the same rating scale for all candidates. This reduces bias and increases validity. The resistance is cultural — interviewers believe they can judge candidates intuitively. They cannot.

# Module 4 — Onboarding

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Onboarding is the process of integrating new employees into the organization — distinct from one-day orientation. Effective onboarding drives employee engagement, reduces turnover, and provides return on investment. The module covers the four stages of socialization (confronting reality, role clarity, locating in organization, detecting signposts), the ROI of onboarding (10:6:2 rule: 10% increase in commitment → 6% increase in effort → 2% increase in performance), practical onboarding strategies (checklists, pre-start communication, first-day planning), Individual Development Plans (IDPs), and integration with performance management.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Onboarding vs. Orientation:** Orientation is first-day paperwork and tour. Onboarding is a comprehensive process (up to one year) helping new hires integrate.

- **Four Stages of Socialization:**
  1. Confronting and accepting organizational reality (culture, strategy)
  2. Achieving role clarity (responsibilities, how to perform)
  3. Locating in organizational context (team, company fit)
  4. Detecting signposts of successful socialization (engagement, contribution, innovation)

- **10:6:2 Rule (ROI of Engagement):** 10% increase in employee commitment → 6% increase in effort → 2% increase in performance.

- **Turnover Costs:** Separation costs, vacancy costs, replacement costs, training costs. Can reach 100-150% of annual salary.

- **Re-recruiting:** The first 90-100 days are critical for retaining new hires by proving mutual value.

- **Individual Development Plan (IDP):** Template for tailoring employee development to individual needs and goals. Goals should be specific, personal, attainable.

- **Drivers of Employee Satisfaction:** Compensation, benefits, job security.

- **Drivers of Both Satisfaction and Engagement:** Relationship with supervisor, opportunities to use skills.

- **Specific Drivers of Engagement:** Positive coworker relationships, nature of work, connection to mission.

- **Manager's Role in Onboarding:** Be honest about management style, give time and attention (daily check-ins first week), assume potential, build relationships, prepare team, set up welcoming environment.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Onboarding is an investment with measurable ROI.** The 10:6:2 rule quantifies the link between commitment, effort, and performance. Retaining engaged employees through good onboarding reduces costly turnover (100-150% of salary).

2. **The first 90 days are "re-recruiting."** This is when new hires decide whether to stay. Prove mutual value early.

3. **Onboarding should be personalized, not one-size-fits-all.** Senior executives, veterans, different generations, and individuals with protected statuses need tailored approaches.

4. **The manager is the most important factor in onboarding.** Daily check-ins the first week, ongoing formal check-ins at 30/60/90 days and beyond. The manager's relationship with the new hire drives engagement.

5. **Onboarding connects to performance management and development.** Discuss performance systems early. Introduce Individual Development Plans (IDPs) during onboarding, revisit during performance reviews.

---

- **Turnover Cost Range:** 100-150% of annual salary

## F. Practical Implications

- Start onboarding before the first day. Personalized communication after offer acceptance sets a positive tone.

- Use a structured onboarding checklist. Include first contact, pre-start information, first day activities, first week, and check-ins at 30/60/90 days.

- Managers should do daily check-ins the first week. This is not micromanagement — it is engagement.

- Introduce Individual Development Plans (IDPs) during onboarding. Goals should be specific, personal, and attainable.

- Prepare the team before the new hire arrives. Assign specific roles for training and social support.

- For remote hires, adapt onboarding: virtual welcome messages, video meetings, virtual tours.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The 10:6:2 rule is a useful way to communicate the ROI of engagement to skeptical executives. A 10% increase in commitment yields a 2% increase in performance — that may not sound like much, but when multiplied across the workforce, it is substantial. And the turnover cost savings (100-150% of salary) are even more compelling.

The "re-recruiting" concept (first 90 days) reframes onboarding as retention, not just paperwork. New hires are deciding whether to stay. Managers who ignore the first 90 days are gambling with turnover.

The manager's role in onboarding is underappreciated. Many managers delegate onboarding to HR or the team. But the manager's relationship with the new hire is the strongest driver of engagement. Daily check-ins the first week, ongoing check-ins at 30/60/90 days — this is not optional.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course provides a complete framework for workforce planning, recruitment, selection, and onboarding. The key insights: workforce planning must align with organizational strategy — the workforce behaviors you need depend on your competitive strategy. Job analysis is the foundation of all HR functions; without it, job descriptions, selection criteria, and performance appraisals are flawed. Internal hires perform better and stay longer than external hires. The best selection tools are work samples, cognitive ability tests, and structured interviews — not unstructured interviews. Biases are pervasive but can be reduced through training and structured processes. Onboarding is an investment with measurable ROI (10:6:2 rule); the first 90 days are "re-recruiting" and managers are the most important factor in retention.

The course's strength is its practical, evidence-based approach. Every claim is supported by research (validity of selection tools, internal vs. external hiring performance, ROI of engagement). The case studies (Google, Zappos, Cisco) and interviews (Colin Partridge, Rand Park) add real-world context. The weakness is that the course is introductory — subsequent courses in the specialization cover performance management and compensation in depth.
